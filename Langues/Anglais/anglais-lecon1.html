📘 1. Present Tenses

English has three main present tenses, each serving a different purpose. Understanding when to use each one is fundamental to communicating effectively in English.

📌 1.1 Present Simple

The Present Simple is used to describe habits, general truths, permanent states, and scheduled events. It is one of the most frequently used tenses in English.

Formation

// Present Simple - Affirmative Form
// Subject + Base Verb (+ s/es for 3rd person singular)
I work. → I work. — General habit
You work. → You work. — General habit
He/She/It works. → He/She works. — 3rd person singular adds -s or -es
We/They work. → We/They work. — General habit
// Spelling Rules for 3rd Person Singular
// Most verbs: add -s work → works | play → plays | read → reads

// Verbs ending in s, x, z, ch, sh, o: add -es pass → passes | watch → watches | go → goes | teach → teaches

// Verbs ending in consonant + y: change y to ies study → studies | fly → flies | try → tries

// Exceptions: have → has
// Present Simple - Negative Form
// Subject + do not (don't) / does not (doesn't) + base verb
I do not (don't) work. — I don't work here.
You do not (don't) work. — You don't need this.
He/She does not (doesn't) work. — She doesn't understand.
We/They do not (don't) work. — They don't agree.
// Present Simple - Interrogative Form
// Do/Does + Subject + Base Verb + ?
Do you work? — Do you work here?
Does she work? — Does she live in Tunis?
Do they speak English? — Do they speak English?

// Short Answers Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
Yes, she does. / No, she doesn't.

Use Cases

📌 When to Use Present Simple

1. Habits and Routines: "I drink coffee every morning." (daily action)

2. General Truths: "Water boils at 100°C." (scientific fact)

3. Permanent States: "She lives in Paris." (permanent situation)

4. Scheduled Events: "The train departs at 8 PM." (timetables)

5. Instructions: "You turn left, then right." (directions)

6. Commentary: "He takes the ball and kicks it!" (sports)

Adverbs of Frequency

🔄

always (100%) — He always arrives on time.

🔄

usually (80-90%) — I usually wake up early.

🔄

often/frequently (60-70%) — She often goes to the gym.

🔄

sometimes (30-50%) — I sometimes eat pizza.

🔄

rarely/seldom (10-20%) — He rarely watches TV.

🔄

never (0%) — I never smoke.

📌 1.2 Present Continuous

The Present Continuous (also called Present Progressive) describes actions happening at the moment of speaking or around the current time period.

// Present Continuous - Formation
// Subject + am/is/are + Verb-ing
I am (I'm) reading. → I'm reading. — happening now
He/She is (He's/She's) working. → She's working. — happening now
We/They are (We're/They're) playing. → They're playing. — happening now
// Present Continuous - All Forms
// Negative: am/is/are + not + verb-ing I am not (I'm not) reading. — I'm not reading. He is not (isn't) listening. — He isn't listening.

// Interrogative: Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing ? Are you listening? — Are you listening? Is she cooking? — Is she cooking? What are you doing? — What are you doing?

Spelling Rules for -ing

// How to Add -ing to Verbs
// Most verbs: simply add -ing work → working | play → playing | read → reading

// Verbs ending in -e: drop -e, add -ing make → making | write → writing | take → taking | drive → driving

// Verbs ending in consonant + vowel + consonant: double final consonant run → running | sit → sitting | stop → stopping | get → getting

// Exception: don't double w, x, y show → showing | fix → fixing | say → saying

📌 Present Simple vs Present Continuous

Present Simple: I work at a bank. → Habit or permanent job

Present Continuous: I am working right now. → Action in progress


Present Simple: She lives in Tunis. → Permanent residence

Present Continuous: She is staying at a hotel. → Temporary situation


Present Simple: I drink coffee every day. → Habit

Present Continuous: I am drinking coffee now. → In progress

⚠️ Important: Stative Verbs

Some verbs describe states (not actions) and are NOT used in continuous tenses:

Emotions: love, like, hate, prefer, dislike, want, wish, desire

Mental states: know, believe, understand, remember, forget, think, realize

Senses: see, hear, smell, taste, feel (when describing perception)

Possession: have, own, possess, belong

Others: be, seem, appear, contain, consist, depend, matter


✓ I love music.     ✗ I am loving music. (WRONG!)

✓ I see the problem.    ✗ I am seeing the problem. (WRONG!)

📌 1.3 Present Perfect

The Present Perfect connects the past with the present. It describes actions or situations that occurred at an unspecified time before now, with relevance to the present moment.

// Present Perfect - Formation
// Subject + have/has + Past Participle
// Regular verbs: verb + ed (same as Past Simple) I have (I've) worked. → I've worked here. — experience
// Irregular verbs: must learn the past participle form She has (She's) seen it. → She's seen the film. — completed action
They have been there. → They've been there. — state until now
// Present Perfect - Negative and Interrogative
// Negative: have/has + not + past participle I haven't finished yet. — I haven't finished yet. She hasn't called. — She hasn't called.

// Interrogative: Have/Has + subject + past participle ? Have you seen it? — Have you seen it? Has she arrived? — Has she arrived? Where have you been? — Where have you been?

📌 Time Markers for Present Perfect

already — already completed: "I've already eaten."

yet — completion expected (neg/question): "Have you finished yet?" / "I haven't finished yet."

just — very recently completed: "I've just arrived."

ever — in someone's life (questions): "Have you ever been to London?"

never — not in someone's life: "I've never eaten sushi."

for — duration (period of time): "I've lived here for 5 years."

since — starting point: "I've lived here since 2019."

Key Differences: Present Perfect vs Past Simple

Present Perfect Past Simple
Unspecified time Specified time (yesterday, last week, in 2020)
Connected to present Finished past
Result important now Action important
"I've seen that movie." (experience) "I saw that movie last night." (specific time)
"She has gone to Paris." (she's there now) "She went to Paris." (completed action)

📌 1.4 Present Perfect Continuous

Used to emphasize the duration of an action that started in the past and is still continuing or has just finished with visible results.

// Present Perfect Continuous
// Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing
I have been working for 3 hours. — duration of action
She has been studying all day. — recently and duration

// Negative: haven't/hasn't + been + verb-ing I haven't been sleeping well lately. — recent problem

// Interrogative: Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing ? Why have you been crying? — visible evidence

📌 Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous

Present Perfect: I have worked here for 5 years. → Emphasizes the experience/duration

Present Perfect Continuous: I have been working here for 5 years. → Emphasizes the ongoing nature


Present Perfect: She has painted the room. → The room is now painted (result)

Present Perfect Continuous: She has been painting the room. → She painted it (activity, might still be painting)

📗 2. Past Tenses

English has four past tenses, each describing different aspects of past time. Understanding when to use each tense is essential for describing events accurately.

📌 2.1 Past Simple

The Past Simple describes completed actions or states that happened at a specific time in the past. It is the most commonly used past tense.

// Past Simple - Affirmative Form
// Regular verbs: verb + ed work → worked | play → played | study → studied | finish → finished

// Irregular verbs: must memorize (no pattern) go → went | see → saw | eat → ate | take → took | write → wrote
have → had | be → was/were | do → did | speak → spoke | give → gave
// Spelling Rules for Regular Verbs
// Most verbs: add -ed work → worked | play → played | help → helped

// Verbs ending in -e: add -d only love → loved | like → liked | create → created

// Verbs ending in consonant + y: change y to ied study → studied | try → tried | cry → cried | marry → married

// Short verbs (1 vowel + 1 consonant): double final consonant stop → stopped | plan → planned | prefer → preferred | occur → occurred
// Past Simple - All Forms
// Affirmative: Subject + verb (past) I worked. — I worked yesterday.
She went home. — She went home.

// Negative: Subject + did not (didn't) + base verb I didn't work. — I didn't work yesterday. She didn't see him. — She didn't see him.

// Interrogative: Did + subject + base verb ? Did you work? — Did you work? Did she go there? — Did she go there? When did you arrive? — When did you arrive?

Past Time Markers

⏰

yesterday — yesterday, yesterday morning

⏰

last — last week, last month, last year

⏰

ago — two days ago, three weeks ago

⏰

in + year — in 2020, in 1995

⏰

when + past — when I was young

⏰

once upon a time — fairy tale opening

📌 2.2 Past Continuous

The Past Continuous describes actions that were in progress at a specific time in the past or that were interrupted by another action.

// Past Continuous - Formation
// Subject + was/were + verb-ing
I/He/She/It was working. — I was working.
You/We/They were playing. — They were playing.
// Past Continuous - All Forms
// Affirmative I was reading when he called. — Action in progress

// Negative: was/were + not + verb-ing I wasn't listening. — I wasn't listening.

// Interrogative: Was/Were + subject + verb-ing ? Were you sleeping? — Were you sleeping?
// Using Past Continuous with Past Simple (Interruption)
// Past Continuous = longer action in progress // Past Simple = shorter interruption
I was reading when he called. — I was reading; then he called (interruption)
She was cooking while I worked. — simultaneous ongoing actions
They were sleeping when the earthquake hit. — sudden event during ongoing action

// Two ongoing actions (using while) While I was studying, she was watching TV. — both in progress simultaneously

📌 2.3 Past Perfect (Past Simple + Past Perfect)

The Past Perfect describes an action that happened BEFORE another action in the past. It is used to show the sequence of past events.

// Past Perfect - Formation
// Subject + had (hadn't) + Past Participle
I had (I'd) finished. — completed before another point
She hadn't arrived. — negative form
// Past Perfect - Usage Examples
// Past Perfect = earlier action // Past Simple = later action
I had finished my homework before I went out. — finished first, then went out
She realized she had forgotten her keys. — forgot first, then realized
When I arrived, the film had already started. — started before my arrival

// With time expressions by the time — By the time I got there, they had left. before — He left before I had spoken to him. after — After she had finished, she left.

📌 2.4 Past Perfect Continuous

Similar to Past Perfect but emphasizes the duration of the action before another past action.

// Past Perfect Continuous
// Subject + had + been + verb-ing
I had been working for 2 hours when he called. — duration before interruption
She had been studying all night before the exam. — long duration before event

📌 Summary: All Past Tenses Comparison

Past Simple: I saw him yesterday. → single completed action

Past Continuous: I was watching TV at 8 PM. → action in progress

Past Perfect: I had seen the movie before. → completed before another past point

Past Perfect Continuous: I had been waiting for 1 hour when he arrived. → duration before another past event

📙 3. Future Tenses and Forms

English has several ways to express the future. The choice depends on the level of certainty, planning, and the context of the prediction or intention.

📌 3.1 Will

"Will" is used for spontaneous decisions, predictions without evidence, promises, and offers.

// Will - Formation
// Affirmative: Subject + will + base verb I will (I'll) help you. — promise/offer

// Negative: will not (won't) I will not (won't) come. — refusal

// Interrogative: Will + subject + base verb ? Will you help me? — offer Will it rain? — prediction

📌 When to Use "Will"

1. Spontaneous Decision: "The phone is ringing. I'll answer it." (decided at the moment)

2. Prediction (no evidence): "I think it will rain tomorrow." (personal belief)

3. Promise: "I will always love you."

4. Offer: "Will you have some tea?" / "I'll help you with that."

5. Threat: "I'll tell the teacher if you don't behave."

6. Refusal: "I won't do it."

📌 3.2 Going to

"Going to" is used for planned intentions and predictions based on visible evidence.

// Going to - Formation
// Subject + am/is/are + going to + verb
I am (I'm) going to study. — planned intention
She is (She's) going to travel. — planned intention

// Negative: am/is/are + not + going to + verb I am not (I'm not) going to go. — refusal of plan

// Interrogative: Am/Is/Are + subject + going to + verb ? Are you going to come? — question about plan

📌 When to Use "Going to"

1. Planned Intentions: "I'm going to visit my grandmother next week." (already decided)

2. Predictions with Evidence: "Look at those clouds! It's going to rain!" (you can see signs)

3. Future Arrangements: "I'm going to start a new job in September."

📌 3.3 Present Continuous for Future

Used for arrangements that are already planned and confirmed.

// Present Continuous for Future
// Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing (with future time reference)
I am meeting John tomorrow. — arranged meeting
She is flying to London next week. — booked flight
They are having a party on Friday. — planned event

Will vs Going to vs Present Continuous

Will Going to Present Continuous
Spontaneous decision Planned intention Fixed arrangement
"I'll call you tonight." "I'm going to call him." "I'm calling him at 6."
Prediction (no evidence) Prediction (with evidence) —
"It will be cold." "It's going to snow!" —

📌 3.4 Future Continuous

Used for actions that will be in progress at a specific future time.

// Future Continuous
// Subject + will + be + verb-ing
This time tomorrow, I will be flying to Paris. — in progress at future time
Don't call at 8 PM — we will be having dinner. — ongoing activity

// Negative: will + not + be + verb-ing I won't be working this weekend. — won't be in progress

📌 3.5 Future Perfect

Used for actions that will be completed before a specific future time.

// Future Perfect
// Subject + will + have + past participle
By 2027, I will have graduated. — completed before 2027
She will have finished by then. — completed before then

📕 4. Modal Verbs

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express possibility, ability, permission, obligation, and other attitudes. They are used with the base form of the main verb (without "to").

📌 Complete Overview of Modal Verbs

Modal Main Use Example Neg/Question
Can Ability, Permission (informal) I can swim. can't / Can I?
Could Past ability, Polite request Could you help? couldn't / Could I?
May Permission (formal), Possibility May I sit? may not / May I?
Might Small possibility It might rain. mightn't / Might I?
Must Strong obligation (internal) You must study. mustn't / Must I?
Have to Obligation (external rule) I have to wear uniform. don't have to
Should Advice, Recommendation You should rest. shouldn't / Should I?
Ought to Advice (similar to should) You ought to try. oughtn't to
Would Hypothetical, Polite request I would like... wouldn't / Would you?

📌 Detailed Uses

Ability

// Can / Could - Ability
// Present ability I can speak three languages. — present ability

// Past ability I could swim when I was 5. — past ability
She could play piano well. — past skill

// Negative: can't (present), couldn't (past) I can't drive. — cannot drive He couldn't attend. — was not able to attend

Permission

// Can / Could / May - Permission
// Informal permission (can) Can I use your phone? — casual

// More polite (could/may) Could I borrow your book? — polite May I sit here? — formal

// Permission granted/refused Yes, you can. / No, I'm afraid not. Yes, you may. / No, you may not.

Possibility

// May / Might / Could - Possibility
// Degrees of possibility May / Might / Could rain later. — maybe it will rain

// Certainty levels (most to least certain) It will rain. (certain) — I'm sure It should rain. (probable) — probably It might rain. (possible) — maybe It may rain. (possible) — maybe It could rain. (possible) — maybe

Obligation

// Must / Have to - Obligation
// Must = internal obligation (you feel it's necessary) I must study harder. — I feel I need to

// Have to = external obligation (rule, law, someone else decides) I have to wear a uniform. — school rule You have to be 18 to vote. — law

// Past obligation I had to work yesterday. — was required to

// Mustn't vs Don't have to You mustn't smoke here. — prohibition (not allowed) You don't have to come. — optional (not required)

Advice and Recommendation

// Should / Ought to - Advice
// Should = general advice (most common) You should see a doctor. — recommendation You shouldn't worry. — negative advice

// Ought to = similar to should (more formal) You ought to apologize. — you should You ought not to have done that. — criticism of past action

// Should have / Ought to have = criticism of past action You should have studied. — you didn't but should have I shouldn't have said that. — I said it but shouldn't have

Polite Requests and Offers

// Would / Could - Polite Requests
// Requests (more polite than will/can) Would you pass the salt? — polite request Could you help me, please? — polite request Can you help me? — less formal

// Offers (Will / Shall) Will you have some tea? — offer Shall I open the window? — offer (mainly British) I'll carry that for you. — offer

Hypothetical Situations

// Would - Hypothetical
// Unreal situations (present/past) If I were rich, I would travel. — present hypothetical If I had studied, I would have passed. — past hypothetical

// Polite/Softening I would like some coffee. — polite way to want I wouldn't mind a vacation. — I would like

// Would like vs Want I would like to order. — polite (restaurant) I want to order. — direct

⚠️ Important Modal Rules

• Modals are followed by the base verb (without "to")

✓ You can go.     ✗ You can to go. (WRONG!)

• Modals do NOT change form for different subjects

✓ He can go.     ✗ He cans go. (WRONG!)

• To form negative, add not after the modal

✓ can't, couldn't, shouldn't, wouldn't, mustn't

• Questions are formed by putting the modal first

✓ Can you help?     ✗ You can help? (WRONG!)

📒 5. Passive Voice

The passive voice is used when the action is more important than the agent (who performed the action), or when the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or obvious.

📌 Formation

// Passive Voice Structure
// Subject + BE + Past Participle (+ by agent)
// Active: Someone stole my bike.
// Passive: My bike was stolen (by someone).

Passive in All Tenses

Tense Active Passive
Present Simple People speak French. French is spoken.
Present Continuous They are painting the house. The house is being painted.
Present Perfect They have built a new school. A new school has been built.
Past Simple Shakespeare wrote this play. This play was written by Shakespeare.
Past Continuous They were building a bridge. A bridge was being built.
Past Perfect They had finished the work. The work had been finished.
Future (will) They will announce the results. The results will be announced.
Future (going to) They are going to hold a meeting. A meeting is going to be held.
Modal verbs You must do the work. The work must be done.

📌 Examples in Context

// Passive Voice Examples
// Present Simple Passive English is spoken in many countries. — worldwide language
French is spoken in Tunisia. — official language

// Past Simple Passive The president was elected last month. — completed action
The city was founded in 698 BC. — historical fact

// Present Perfect Passive The movie has been watched millions. — popular movie
My phone has been stolen. — recent event

// With "by agent" The letter was written by my mother. — agent mentioned
The Mona Lisa was painted by Da Vinci. — famous painter

📌 When to Use Passive Voice

1. Agent is unknown: "My car was stolen." (who took it is unknown)

2. Agent is irrelevant: "Water is boiled at 100°C." (who boils it doesn't matter)

3. Agent is less important: "Many buildings were destroyed in the earthquake."

4. Formality: "Applications must be submitted by Friday."

5. Topic focus: "The discovery was made in 1928." (the discovery is the topic)

📓 6. Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences express "if" situations and their possible results. There are four main types, each referring to different time frames and levels of probability.

📌 The Four Types of Conditionals

Type 0: Zero Conditional

Used for general truths and facts that are always true when the condition is met.

// Zero Conditional - General Truths
// Structure: If + Present Simple, Present Simple
If you heat water, it boils. — scientific fact
If it rains, the ground gets wet. — general truth
Water freezes if it gets cold enough. — always true

// Alternative: Can use when/whenever instead of if When you mix red and blue, you get purple. — general result

Type 1: First Conditional

Used for real and likely situations in the present or future.

// First Conditional - Real/Likely Situations
// Structure: If + Present Simple, will + base verb
If it rains, I will stay home. — possible future event
If you study hard, you will pass. — likely outcome

// Can also use: might, can, may, should If you see her, you should tell her. — advice
If you need help, you can call me. — offer

// Alternative structures (will can be in either clause) If I have time, I will help you. — most common I will help you if I have time. — same meaning

Type 2: Second Conditional

Used for unreal, imaginary, or unlikely situations in the present or future.

// Second Conditional - Unreal/Hypothetical Situations
// Structure: If + Past Simple (were or past verb), would + base verb
If I had a million dollars, I would buy a yacht. — hypothetical present
If I were you, I would accept. — unreal situation

// Note: "If I were" is more common than "If I was" If I were rich, I would travel the world. — imaginary

// Would can be replaced with might or could If I studied more, I could pass. — possibility

Type 3: Third Conditional

Used for unreal situations in the past — things that did not happen.

// Third Conditional - Unreal Past Situations
// Structure: If + Past Perfect, would have + past participle
If I had studied, I would have passed. — didn't study, didn't pass
If she had left earlier, she would have caught the train. — didn't leave, didn't catch

// Might have or could have instead of would have If he had tried, he might have succeeded. — possible outcome

// Mixed conditional (past condition → present result) If I had worked harder, I would be a manager now. — past action → present state

Summary Table

Type If clause Main clause Use
Zero Present Simple Present Simple General truths
First Present Simple will + verb Likely future
Second Past Simple (were) would + verb Unlikely present/future
Third Past Perfect would have + past participle Imaginary past

💡 Mixed Conditionals Examples

Past → Present: If I had taken the job, I would be rich now. (but I didn't take it)

Present → Past: If I were braver, I would have spoken up. (but I'm not brave enough)

📘 7. Linking Words and Connectors

Linking words (also called connectors or transition words) are essential for creating coherent and well-structured writing. They show the relationship between ideas.

📌 Addition (Adding Ideas)

Connector Usage Example
and Same type of idea She is smart and talented.
also / too / as well Adding similar information She speaks English. She also speaks French.
in addition (to) Formal addition In addition to studying, she works part-time.
furthermore / moreover Strong addition The food was good. Furthermore, the service was excellent.
besides Adding a point Besides being cheap, it's also delicious.
not only... but also Emphatic addition Not only is it cheap, but also it's healthy.

📌 Contrast (Opposite Ideas)

Connector Usage Example
but Simple contrast It's cheap, but it's not good.
however / nevertheless Formal contrast It was raining. However, we went out.
although / though / even though Concession (despite) Although it was raining, we went out.
despite / in spite of Concession (noun/gerund) Despite the rain, we went out.
on the other hand Presenting opposite view On the other hand, some people prefer rural life.
while / whereas Comparing two things While city life is exciting, whereas rural life is peaceful.
unlike Showing difference Unlike her sister, she is very outgoing.
still / yet Unexpected contrast It was expensive. Still, I bought it.

📌 Cause and Effect (Reason/Result)

Connector Usage Example
because Reason (before result) I stayed home because it was raining.
since / as Reason (more formal) Since you are here, let's begin.
so Result (before reason) It was raining, so I stayed home.
therefore / consequently Formal result He studied hard. Therefore, he passed.
as a result Result He didn't study. As a result, he failed.
because of / due to Cause (noun) Because of the rain, we stayed home.
owing to Formal cause Owing to the weather, the match was cancelled.

📌 Sequence and Time (Ordering Ideas)

Connector Usage Example
first(ly) / first of all Beginning First, gather your ingredients.
second(ly) / third(ly) Middle points Secondly, mix the dry ingredients.
then / next Next step Then, add water.
after that / afterwards Following step After that, let it rest for 10 minutes.
finally / lastly / last but not least End point Finally, serve and enjoy.
meanwhile Simultaneous action Meanwhile, prepare the sauce.

📌 Examples and Illustration

Connector Usage Example
for example / for instance Illustrating a point Many jobs require English. For example, in tourism.
such as / like Giving examples I like fruits such as apples and oranges.
in fact / actually Emphasizing truth In fact, English is the global language.
especially / particularly Focusing on specifics All subjects are important, especially mathematics.

📌 Conclusion and Summary

Connector Usage Example
in conclusion / to conclude Ending an essay In conclusion, technology has both pros and cons.
to sum up / in summary Summarizing To sum up, we need to take action now.
in short / in brief Concise summary In short, the project was a success.
overall / all in all General assessment Overall, it was a great experience.
in my opinion / personally Giving personal view In my opinion, this is the best solution.

💡 Sample Paragraph with Connectors

Topic: The advantages and disadvantages of social media

Social media has become an integral part of modern life. On the one hand, it offers numerous benefits. Firstly, it allows people to stay connected with friends and family across the world. Furthermore, it provides a platform for learning and sharing information. For example, students can access educational content online.

On the other hand, there are also significant drawbacks. Although social media can be beneficial, however, excessive use can lead to addiction. Moreover, privacy concerns have increased because personal data can be misused. As a result, many users experience anxiety and depression.

In conclusion, while social media has its advantages, it is crucial to use it responsibly. In my opinion, we should establish healthy boundaries to maximize the benefits while minimizing the risks.

📗 8. Reported Speech

Reported speech (also called indirect speech) is used to report what someone said without using their exact words. The tense usually shifts back in time.

📌 Direct vs Reported Speech

// Direct Speech vs Reported Speech
// Direct Speech (exact words) "I am tired," she said.

// Reported Speech (paraphrased) She said that she was tired.

Tense Changes (Backshift)

Direct Speech Reported Speech
Present Simple → Past Simple
Present Continuous → Past Continuous
Present Perfect → Past Perfect
Past Simple → Past Perfect
will → would
can → could
may → might
// Tense Backshift Examples
// Present Simple → Past Simple "I like coffee." → She said she liked coffee.

// Present Continuous → Past Continuous "I am working." → He said he was working.

// Present Perfect → Past Perfect "I have finished." → She said she had finished.

// Past Simple → Past Perfect "I saw her." → He said he had seen her.

// will → would "I will call you." → She said she would call me.

// can → could "I can help." → He said he could help.

Time Expression Changes

// Time Expression Shifts
// When reporting in the past today → that day yesterday → the day before / the previous day tomorrow → the next day / the following day this week → that week last week → the week before next week → the following week now → then here → there ago → before

Say vs Tell vs Ask

// Reporting Verbs
// tell + object "I am tired," she told me. → She told me that she was tired.

// say (no object needed) "I am tired," she said. → She said (that) she was tired.

// ask + object (questions) "Are you coming?" he asked. → He asked if I was coming. "Where do you live?" she asked. → She asked where I lived.

Reporting Questions

// Yes/No Questions
// if / whether "Are you coming?" → She asked if I was coming. "Do you understand?" → He asked whether I understood.

// Wh- Questions (keep the question word)
"Where do you live?" → She asked where I lived. "What time is it?" → He asked what time it was. "Why did you leave?" → She asked why I had left.

// Note: Question word order in reported questions "Where are you going?" → She asked where I was going. (not "where was I going")

Reporting Commands and Requests

// Commands and Requests
// tell/ask + object + to + infinitive
// Imperative → Infinitive construction "Close the door." → She told me to close the door. "Please sit down." → He asked me to sit down.

// Negative commands "Don't touch that." → She told me not to touch that. "Don't worry." → He asked me not to worry.

📙 9. Relative Clauses

Relative clauses are used to add additional information about a noun without starting a new sentence. They begin with relative pronouns.

📌 Relative Pronouns

Pronoun Use Example
who People (subject) The student who passed was happy.
whom People (object) - formal The teacher whom we respect is leaving.
which Things and animals The book which I read was interesting.
that People and things (defining) The book that I bought was expensive.
whose Possession The student whose work was best got a prize.
where Places The city where I live is beautiful.
when Time The day when I met you was amazing.
why Reasons The reason why I called was to invite you.

📌 Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Defining Relative Clauses (Essential)

These clauses are essential for understanding the sentence. They identify which person or thing is being referred to. No commas are used.

// Defining Clauses - No Commas
The student who studies hard will pass. — identifies which student
The book that I bought is interesting. — identifies which book
The man whose car broke down called for help. — possession

// With prepositions The book which I was looking for was expensive. (for = preposition)
The student whom I told you about passed. (about = preposition)

Non-Defining Relative Clauses (Non-Essential)

These clauses add extra information that is not essential. They are separated by commas and can be removed without changing the meaning.

// Non-Defining Clauses - With Commas
My brother, who lives in Paris, is visiting. — extra info
Mrs. Smith, whom you met yesterday, is our teacher. — extra info
Tunisia, which is a North African country, has beautiful beaches. — additional fact

// IMPORTANT: Don't use "that" in non-defining clauses! My brother, who lives in Paris... (✓)    My brother, that lives in Paris... (✗)

Relative Pronouns: Subject vs Object

// Subject vs Object Pronouns
// Subject pronoun: acts as subject in the relative clause who/which + verb
The student who passed the exam was happy. (who = subject of "passed")
The book which costs 50 dinars is mine. (which = subject of "costs")

// Object pronoun: can often be omitted in defining clauses who(m)/that/which + subject + verb
The student who(m) I met was nice. = The student I met was nice. (omitted)
The book that I bought is expensive. = The book I bought is expensive.

// Object pronoun CANNOT be omitted in non-defining clauses My brother, whom I love, is here. (whom cannot be omitted)

⚠️ Relative Pronoun Rules

• Use who for people (subject) — "The man who called was tall."

• Use whom for people (object, formal) — "The man whom I saw was tall."

• Use which for things and animals

• Use that for defining clauses (people AND things)

• That is NOT used in non-defining clauses

• Object pronouns can be omitted in defining clauses

• Whose shows possession (no apostrophe) — "the student whose book was lost"

📕 10. Common Mistakes and Practice Exercises

This section covers the most common errors students make and provides extensive practice exercises to help you master English grammar.

📌 Top 20 Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Mistake 1: Subject-Verb Agreement

✗ She don't understand.    ✓ She doesn't understand.

✗ The book are on the table.    ✓ The book is on the table.

⚠️ Mistake 2: Articles (a/an/the)

✗ I am a honest person.    ✓ I am an honest person. (an before vowel sound)

✗ I went to school yesterday.    ✓ Correct as is. (no article with places like school, hospital, work, church when their purpose)

⚠️ Mistake 3: Countable vs Uncountable Nouns

✗ I have many informations.    ✓ I have much information. (information is uncountable)

✗ Give me some advice.    ✓ Give me some advice. (advice is uncountable)

⚠️ Mistake 4: Word Order in Questions

✗ Where you are going?    ✓ Where are you going?

✗ What does he means?    ✓ What does he mean? (base verb after auxiliary)

⚠️ Mistake 5: Using gerunds vs infinitives

✗ I enjoy to read.    ✓ I enjoy reading. (enjoy + -ing)

✗ I decided going.    ✓ I decided to go. (decide + to + verb)

⚠️ Mistake 6: Past Simple vs Present Perfect

✗ I have seen him yesterday.    ✓ I saw him yesterday. (yesterday = Past Simple)

✗ I have visited Paris in 2020.    ✓ I visited Paris in 2020. (specific past time = Past Simple)

⚠️ Mistake 7: Silent Letters

✗ I wrote (pronounced /roʊt/)    ✓ write (/raɪt/)

English has many silent letters: knife, hour, honest, castle, Wednesday

⚠️ Mistake 8: Because vs Because of

✗ Because of it was raining, we stayed home.    ✓ Because it was raining, we stayed home.

✗ Because the rain, we stayed home.    ✓ Because of the rain, we stayed home. (because of + noun)

⚠️ Mistake 9: Like vs As/Like vs Such as

✗ Like I said before...    ✓ As I said before... (like = similar to; as = in the capacity of)

✗ I have hobbies like reading and swimming.    ✓ I have hobbies such as reading and swimming.

⚠️ Mistake 10: Make vs Do

✗ Let's make homework.    ✓ Let's do homework. (do = perform tasks)

✗ She does a mistake.    ✓ She made a mistake. (make = create)
