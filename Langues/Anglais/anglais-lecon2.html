📘 1. Understanding Question Types

Before you can answer a question correctly, you must first understand what type of question it is and what kind of answer is expected. The BAC Tunisia exam typically includes several categories of questions.

📌 Category A: Global Comprehension Questions

These questions test your overall understanding of the text. They ask about the main idea, the author's purpose, or the general topic.

Common Global Comprehension Questions

• What is the text about? — Identify the main topic

• What is the main idea of the text? — The central point the author wants to convey

• What is the author's purpose? — To inform, to persuade, to entertain, to describe

• What is the most suitable title for the text? — Choose the best heading

• What is the text mainly concerned with? — Main theme

• The text is mainly intended for... — Target audience

Example and Model Answer

// Sample Global Comprehension Question
// Text: "Social media has revolutionized how people communicate. Platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter have created new ways for people to stay connected across the globe. However, this digital transformation has also raised concerns about privacy and mental health."

// Question: What is the text mainly about?

// Model Answer: The text is mainly about the impact of social media on communication, including both its benefits and concerns.

📌 Category B: Detailed Comprehension Questions

These questions test your understanding of specific information in the text. They require you to find and understand particular details.

Common Detailed Comprehension Questions

• According to the text... — Find stated information

• How/Why/When/Where... — Specific details

• What does the word/phrase "X" refer to? — Reference questions

• What is mentioned as... — List-type questions

• Which of the following is stated in the text? — Fact-checking

• How many/much? — Quantities

// Sample Detailed Question
// Text: "According to the World Health Organization, over 3.6 billion people use social media worldwide. This number is expected to grow to 4.9 billion by 2027. The most popular platform is Facebook, followed by YouTube and Instagram."

// Question 1: How many people use social media according to the WHO?
Answer: According to the WHO, over 3.6 billion people use social media worldwide.

// Question 2: What is the expected number of users by 2027?
Answer: The number is expected to grow to 4.9 billion by 2027.

📌 Category C: Vocabulary Questions

These questions test your vocabulary knowledge and ability to understand words in context.

Common Vocabulary Questions

• What does the underlined word/phrase mean? — Contextual meaning

• Find in the text words that are similar in meaning to... — Synonyms

• Which word in the text is opposite in meaning to... — Antonyms

• Choose the correct definition for the word "X" — Multiple choice vocabulary

• Which word could replace "X" without changing the meaning? — Substitutes

📌 Category D: Inference Questions

These questions require you to read between the lines and understand information that is implied but not directly stated.

Common Inference Questions

• It can be inferred from the text that... — Implied meaning

• What can we conclude from the text? — Drawing conclusions

• The author implies that... — Implication

• What is likely to happen? — Prediction

• Why do you think... — Reasoning from evidence

// Sample Inference Question
// Text: "Many teenagers spend over 6 hours daily on their smartphones. Schools have started implementing policies to limit phone use during class hours. Some parents are worried about the impact on their children's academic performance."

// Question: What can be inferred from this text?

// Answer: It can be inferred that excessive smartphone use among teenagers is becoming a concern for both schools and parents, particularly regarding academic performance.

📗 2. Strategies for Finding Answers

Effective reading strategies are crucial for quickly and accurately finding answers in the text. Follow these systematic approaches to maximize your comprehension.

📌 Step-by-Step Reading Strategy

// The 3-Pass Reading Strategy
// FIRST PASS: Skimming (2-3 minutes)
1. Read the title and any subtitle
2. Read the first and last paragraphs
3. Look at any pictures, charts, or headings
4. Get a general understanding of the topic

// SECOND PASS: Scanning (4-5 minutes)
1. Read the questions carefully
2. Look for keywords in the questions
3. Scan the text for those keywords
4. Read the relevant paragraphs more carefully

// THIRD PASS: Careful Reading (as needed)
1. Read the specific sections again
2. Understand the context of each answer
3. Formulate your answers in your own words

📌 How to Identify Keywords

Keywords are the most important words in a question that help you locate the answer in the text.

Question Word Look for in Text Type of Answer
What Nouns, actions, events A thing, concept, or action
Who Names, titles, descriptions of people A person's name or role
Where Place names, locations A place or location
When Dates, time expressions A time, date, or period
Why Because, reason, cause words A reason or explanation
How Method, manner, by + action A method, process, or way
How many Numbers, quantities A number
How much Numbers, prices, uncountable quantities An amount or price
To what extent Degree words, extent expressions Level or degree

📌 Signal Words to Look For

These words often indicate important information in the text:

🔑

Definition signals: is, means, refers to, is called, is defined as

🔑

Example signals: for example, for instance, such as, including, especially

🔑

Reason signals: because, since, due to, the reason is, in order to

🔑

Result signals: therefore, as a result, consequently, so, thus

🔑

Contrast signals: however, although, but, on the other hand, unlike

🔑

Addition signals: moreover, furthermore, in addition, also, besides

🔑

Sequence signals: first, second, then, finally, next, after

🔑

Emphasis signals: importantly, especially, particularly, notably

📌 Time Management Tips

Question Type Recommended Time Strategy
Global comprehension 2-3 minutes Quick scan of whole text
Detailed questions 2-3 minutes each Scan for specific keywords
Vocabulary questions 1-2 minutes each Context analysis
Inference questions 3-4 minutes Careful reading of related parts
True/False questions 1-2 minutes each Find the relevant statement

✅ Pro Tips

• Always read the questions BEFORE the text — This helps you know what to look for

• Underline key information while reading the text

• Mark paragraphs with numbers to find information quickly

• Don't panic if you don't understand every word — focus on what you need to answer

• Watch the time — Don't spend too long on one question

📙 3. How to Paraphrase and Reformulate

Paraphrasing means restating information from the text in your own words. This is a crucial skill for the BAC exam as it shows your understanding of the material and avoids direct copying.

📌 Why Paraphrase?

📝

Demonstrates understanding: You show you truly comprehend the text, not just memorize it

📝

Avoids plagiarism: Direct copying may be penalized

📝

Shows language skills: You demonstrate your vocabulary and grammar knowledge

📝

Adapts to questions: Some questions specifically ask for your own words

📌 Technique 1: Synonym Replacement

The simplest paraphrasing technique involves replacing words with synonyms while maintaining the meaning.

// Synonym Replacement Examples
// Original: "The smartphone has become essential in our daily lives."

// Paraphrased: "The smartphone has become necessary/vital/crucial in our everyday lives."

// Original: "Social media has changed the way we communicate."

// Paraphrased: "Social media has transformed/altered/modified the way we communicate."

// Original: "Many students use phones for educational purposes."

// Paraphrased: "Many students utilize/employ phones for educational purposes."

Useful Synonyms for Academic Writing

Common Word Synonyms
important significant, crucial, essential, vital, key
big/large major, significant, considerable, substantial
many numerous, several, a number of, countless
good beneficial, advantageous, positive, valuable
bad harmful, negative, detrimental, damaging
think believe, consider, feel, argue, maintain
show demonstrate, illustrate, indicate, reveal
use utilize, employ, apply, make use of
help assist, support, aid, facilitate
different various, diverse, distinct, various

📌 Technique 2: Change Word Class (Part of Speech)

Transform words from one part of speech to another while keeping the meaning.

// Word Class Changes
// Verb → Noun
The government implemented new policies. → The government implemented implementation of new policies.

// Adjective → Noun
The study showed positive results. → The study showed positivity in its results.

// Noun → Verb
The development of technology is rapid. → Technology developed rapidly.

// Verb → Adjective
The results surprised everyone. → The results were surprising to everyone.

📌 Technique 3: Change Structure

Rephrase sentences by changing their grammatical structure.

// Structural Changes
// Active → Passive
Original: "Researchers discovered a new treatment."
Paraphrased: "A new treatment was discovered by researchers."

// Direct object → Subject
Original: "The study covered 1000 participants."
Paraphrased: "The study involved 1000 participants."

// Negative → Affirmative
Original: "The problem is not simple."
Paraphrased: "The problem is complex/complicated."

// Simple sentence → Complex
Original: "Technology advanced quickly."
Paraphrased: "Technology advanced at a rapid pace."

📌 Technique 4: Combine Information

Sometimes you can paraphrase by combining related pieces of information.

// Combining Information
// Original text: "The internet has changed education. Students can now access online courses. They can learn from anywhere in the world."

// Paraphrased: "The internet has transformed education by enabling students to access online courses from anywhere in the world."

⚠️ IMPORTANT: What NOT to Do

• NE copy the exact sentence from the text — this is considered plagiarism

• NE change just a few words — this is not true paraphrasing

• NE change the meaning — your paraphrase must accurately reflect the original

• NE add information not found in the text

• DO keep the essential meaning and key facts

📕 4. True/False Questions with Justification

True/False questions require you to determine whether a statement agrees with the text and, in many cases, provide a justification using information from the text.

📌 Question Formats

Format 1: Simple True/False

Simply state whether the statement is True or False.

Example: "Social media is mentioned as a communication tool. (True / False)"

Format 2: True/False with Justification

State True/False AND provide evidence from the text to support your answer.

Example: "According to the text, teenagers spend more than 6 hours on phones daily. (True / False) Justify."

Format 3: True/False/Not Stated

Determine if the statement is explicitly confirmed, denied, or not mentioned in the text.

Example: "The text states that the government has banned smartphones in schools. (True / False / Not Stated)"

📌 How to Answer True/False Questions

// Step-by-Step Approach
// Step 1: Read the statement carefully
Identify the key claim or fact in the statement.

// Step 2: Scan the text for relevant information
Look for keywords related to the statement.

// Step 3: Compare the statement with the text
Check if the information matches, contradicts, or is absent.

// Step 4: Provide your answer with justification
State True/False and quote relevant text evidence.

📌 Model Answers for True/False Questions

// Sample Text
// "Climate change is becoming one of the most pressing challenges of our time. Global temperatures have risen by 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era. This warming is primarily caused by human activities, especially the burning of fossil fuels. The consequences include rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and the loss of biodiversity."

💡 Question 1: True/False with justification

Statement: Global temperatures have increased by 1.1°C since the industrial era.

Answer: True. The text explicitly states that "Global temperatures have risen by 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era."

💡 Question 2: True/False with justification

Statement: Climate change is only caused by natural factors.

Answer: False. The text clearly indicates that climate change is "primarily caused by human activities, especially the burning of fossil fuels," contradicting the statement.

💡 Question 3: True/False/Not Stated

Statement: Governments have implemented policies to reduce carbon emissions.

Answer: Not Stated. While the text discusses the causes and consequences of climate change, it does not mention any government policies or measures to address the issue.

💡 Question 4: True/False with justification

Statement: One consequence of climate change is the loss of biodiversity.

Answer: True. The text lists "the loss of biodiversity" as one of the consequences of climate change, along with rising sea levels and extreme weather events.

📌 Common Patterns to Watch For

Statement Uses Text Uses Answer
"many" "over 3.6 billion" True (many = a large number)
"always" "often" False (different degree)
"can" "must" False (different modality)
"all" "most" False (different scope)
"positive" "negative" False (opposite)
"may" "will" Depends on context

📒 5. Multiple Choice Questions

Multiple choice questions present you with several answer options, and you must select the correct one based on information from the text.

📌 Types of Multiple Choice Questions

Type 1: Single Best Answer

Choose only ONE correct answer from the options provided.

Example: "What is the main purpose of the text?"

a) To entertain readers

b) To inform about climate change

c) To persuade people to travel

d) To describe a vacation

Type 2: Multiple Answers

Select ALL correct answers (may have more than one correct option).

Example: "Which of the following are mentioned as consequences of climate change?"

a) Rising sea levels

b) Economic growth

c) Extreme weather events

d) Loss of biodiversity

Type 3: Vocabulary in Context

Choose the correct meaning of an underlined word based on context.

Example: "What does the word 'crucial' in line 5 mean?"

a) Important

b) Unimportant

c) Difficult

d) Simple

📌 Strategies for Answering Multiple Choice

// Elimination Strategy
// Step 1: Read the question and options carefully
Identify what the question is asking for.

// Step 2: Eliminate obviously wrong answers
Cross out options that are clearly incorrect or contradict the text.

// Step 3: Compare remaining options with the text
Look for the option that best matches the information.

// Step 4: Choose the best answer
Select the option that is most supported by the text.

📌 Common Wrong Answer Traps

⚠️ Trap 1: True but Not Answer the Question

The statement may be true according to the text, but it doesn't answer the specific question asked.

Example: Question asks about CAUSES, but you choose an answer about CONSEQUENCES

⚠️ Trap 2: Word-for-Word from Text

Answers that are copied directly from the text are often incorrect because they don't address the question.

Solution: Make sure the answer actually responds to what is being asked

⚠️ Trap 3: Extreme Words

Options with words like "always," "never," "all," "none," "impossible" are often wrong.

Solution: Look for more moderate language like "sometimes," "may," "could"

⚠️ Trap 4: Similar but Incorrect

Some options look correct but have subtle differences that make them wrong.

Example: "increased by 10%" vs "increased to 10%"

⚠️ Trap 5: Outside Knowledge

Don't use your own knowledge if it contradicts the text. Always base your answer on the text.

Example: You know something is false in reality, but the text states it — follow the text!

📌 Model Answer for Multiple Choice

// Sample Multiple Choice Question
// Text: "Online learning has grown significantly in recent years. Students can now access courses from top universities without leaving their homes. However, this form of education requires strong self-discipline and motivation."

// Question: According to the text, online learning ____.

A) eliminates the need for self-discipline
B) requires self-discipline and motivation
C) is only available to rich students
D) has decreased in popularity

// Analysis:
A) WRONG — The text says it REQUIRES self-discipline, not eliminates it
B) CORRECT — Matches "requires strong self-discipline and motivation"
C) WRONG — Not mentioned in the text
D) WRONG — Text says it has GROWN, not decreased

// Answer: B

📓 6. Vocabulary Questions (Synonyms and Antonyms)

Vocabulary questions test your ability to understand words in context and find their meanings using the surrounding text.

📌 Types of Vocabulary Questions

Type 1: Synonym Questions

// Synonym Question Patterns
// Pattern 1: "The underlined word in line X is closest in meaning to:"

// Pattern 2: "Find in the text a word that is similar in meaning to:"

// Pattern 3: "Which word could replace 'X' without changing the meaning?"

Type 2: Antonym Questions

// Antonym Question Patterns
// Pattern 1: "The underlined word in line X is opposite in meaning to:"

// Pattern 2: "Which word in the text is opposite in meaning to:"

Type 3: Contextual Meaning

// Contextual Meaning Patterns
// Pattern 1: "What does the word 'X' mean in the context of the text?"

// Pattern 2: "In line X, 'X' could be best defined as:"

// Pattern 3: "The word 'X' in paragraph 2 is closest in meaning to:"

📌 How to Find Meaning from Context

// Context Clues Strategy
// Step 1: Read the sentence with the unknown word
Try to understand the overall meaning of the sentence.

// Step 2: Look for context clues
• Definition clues: "The word means, that is, in other words"
• Example clues: "for example, such as, including"
• Contrast clues: "however, but, unlike, although"
• Cause/Effect clues: "because, therefore, as a result"

// Step 3: Make an educated guess
Based on the surrounding context, guess the meaning.

// Step 4: Verify your guess
Check if your guess makes sense in the sentence.

📌 Context Clue Examples

💡 Example 1: Definition Clue

Sentence: "A pandemic, that is, a worldwide epidemic, has affected millions of people."

Meaning: "Pandemic" means a worldwide epidemic (disease spreading worldwide).

💡 Example 2: Contrast Clue

Sentence: "Unlike his introvert brother, John is quite extrovert and loves parties."

Meaning: "Extrovert" is the opposite of introvert (someone who is outgoing and social).

💡 Example 3: Example Clue

Sentence: "Healthy habits include eating vegetables, exercising regularly, and getting enough sleep."

Meaning: "Healthy habits" are behaviors that contribute to good health.

💡 Example 4: Cause/Effect Clue

Sentence: "Due to the heavy rain, the floods caused significant damage to the city."

Meaning: "Floods" are caused by heavy rain and result in water damage.

📘 7. WH-Questions: Detailed Analysis

WH-questions (questions starting with What, Who, Where, When, Why, How, etc.) require specific types of answers. Understanding what each question word asks for will help you answer correctly.

📌 Question Words and Their Functions

Question Word Asks for Example Answer Type
What A thing, action, or fact "A new policy" / "Studying languages"
What + noun Specific type "What kind of" → a specific category
What + N Quantity "What percentage" → a number or percentage
Who A person "The president" / "Researchers"
Whose Possession "My friend's" / "The school's"
Where A place or location "In Paris" / "At the university"
When A time or date "Last year" / "In 2020" / "Every day"
Why A reason or cause "Because of..." / "In order to..."
How A manner, method, or way "By using..." / "In a way that..."
How + adj/adv Degree or extent "How many" → a number

📌 Detailed Analysis of Each Question Type

WHAT Questions

// WHAT Question Patterns
// Pattern 1: What + noun
What is the main idea of the text? → A statement of the central point
What are the benefits mentioned? → A list of items

// Pattern 2: What + verb/action
What happened in 2020? → An event
What can be done about this? → A solution or recommendation

// Pattern 3: What does X mean?
What does the author mean by...? → An explanation

WHO Questions

// WHO Question Patterns
// WHO asks about people (subject or object)
Who conducted the study? → The researcher(s)
Who is mentioned as an example? → A person mentioned
Who does the pronoun "they" refer to? → The people "they" represents

// WHO vs WHOM
Who did this? (subject) → Who performed the action?
Whom did you meet? (object) → Who received the action? (formal)

WHY Questions

// WHY Question Patterns
// WHY asks for reasons, causes, or purposes
Why is this happening? → Because of [reason]
Why did the author write this? → For [purpose]

// Common reason markers in text:
• because (of) — reason
• in order to / so that — purpose
• due to / owing to — cause
• the reason is — explanation

HOW Questions

// HOW Question Patterns
// HOW asks for manner, method, or degree
How does this work? → A method or process
How many? → A number (countable)
How much? → An amount (uncountable)
How long? → Duration (time)
How far? → Distance
How old? → Age
How often? → Frequency
How quickly? → Speed

📌 Reference Questions (What does X refer to?)

These questions ask you to identify what a pronoun, word, or phrase refers to in the context.

// Pronoun Reference
// Text: "Many students struggle with exams. They often feel anxious before tests."

// Question: What does "They" in line 2 refer to?

// Answer: "They" refers to students.
// Word/Phrase Reference
// Text: "Online learning offers flexibility. This is particularly beneficial for working adults."

// Question: What does "This" in line 2 refer to?

// Answer: "This" refers to the flexibility that online learning offers.

📗 8. Error Analysis and Common Mistakes

Understanding the most common errors will help you avoid them and provide better answers on exam day.

📌 Top 15 Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Mistake 1: Answering Based on General Knowledge

Error: "I know this from outside the text, so it must be true."

Reality: Even if something is true in reality, the question asks about the TEXT. If the text doesn't say it, it's not the correct answer.

Example: You know climate change is real, but if the text only mentions rising temperatures, you can only answer based on what the text states.

⚠️ Mistake 2: Partial Understanding

Error: "I think I understand, so I'll answer quickly without checking."

Reality: Always verify your understanding by re-reading the relevant part of the text.

Solution: Take the time to locate and verify the answer in the text.

⚠️ Mistake 3: Misreading the Question

Error: Reading "What is NOT mentioned" as "What is mentioned" or vice versa.

Reality: The word NOT completely changes the answer.

Solution: Underline the word NOT when you see it and make sure to answer accordingly.

⚠️ Mistake 4: Overlooking Negative Words

Error: Missing words like "never," "not," "no," "without," "rarely."

Reality: These words completely change the meaning of a statement.

Solution: Pay special attention to negative words in statements.

⚠️ Mistake 5: Choosing the First Match

Error: Selecting an answer that contains words from the text without fully understanding the question.

Reality: Sometimes the answer with text words is deliberately wrong.

Solution: Always verify that the answer actually addresses the question.

⚠️ Mistake 6: Too Vague or Too Specific Answers

Error: Giving an answer that is either too general or too narrow.

Reality: The answer should be neither too general nor too specific.

Solution: Find the balance — answer exactly what is asked, no more, no less.

⚠️ Mistake 7: Forgetting to Justify

Error: Only writing "True" or "False" without providing evidence.

Reality: Even when not explicitly required, justification strengthens your answer.

Solution: Always provide a brief justification using information from the text.

⚠️ Mistake 8: Making Assumptions

Error: Assuming what happened before or after the text.

Reality: Only base your answers on information IN the text.

Solution: Stick strictly to what is written in the passage.

⚠️ Mistake 9: Ignoring Context

Error: Giving the dictionary meaning of a word instead of the contextual meaning.

Reality: Words can have different meanings depending on context.

Solution: Always derive the meaning from the surrounding context.

⚠️ Mistake 10: Answering in the Wrong Language

Error: Writing answers in French when the question is in English.

Reality: Answers should be in English.

Solution: Always answer in English to demonstrate your language skills.

📌 Quality Check for Your Answers

✅ Before Submitting, Check:

□ Does my answer address exactly what the question asks?

□ Is my answer based solely on information from the text?

□ Have I provided justification where needed?

□ Did I notice any negative words (NOT, never, rarely)?

□ Is my answer clear and complete?

□ Did I use my own words or paraphrase when needed?

□ Are there any spelling or grammar mistakes?

📌 Practice Text 1: Technology and Education

Reading Passage

"The integration of technology in education has transformed traditional learning methods. Today's classrooms are equipped with smart boards, tablets, and high-speed internet, enabling teachers to deliver interactive lessons. According to a recent UNESCO report, over 60% of students worldwide now use digital devices for learning purposes.

Online learning platforms have democratized education by providing access to courses from prestigious universities for free or at low cost. Students from remote areas can now attend virtual classes taught by expert instructors. However, this digital shift has raised concerns about screen time and its impact on students' health.

Experts recommend balancing technology use with traditional learning activities. Physical books, face-to-face interactions, and outdoor activities remain essential for a well-rounded education. The key is to use technology as a tool to enhance learning, not to replace human connection entirely."

Questions

1. What is the text mainly about?

Answer: The text is mainly about how technology has transformed education, its benefits in terms of access to learning, and the importance of balancing it with traditional methods.


2. According to the text, what percentage of students use digital devices for learning?

Answer: According to the UNESCO report mentioned in the text, over 60% of students worldwide use digital devices for learning purposes.


3. How has online learning changed access to education?

Answer: Online learning has made education more accessible by providing courses from prestigious universities for free or at low cost, allowing students from remote areas to attend virtual classes taught by expert instructors.


4. What concerns have been raised about the digital shift in education?

Answer: The text mentions concerns about screen time and its impact on students' health as a result of the increased use of digital devices in education.


5. What do experts recommend?

Answer: Experts recommend balancing technology use with traditional learning activities, emphasizing that physical books, face-to-face interactions, and outdoor activities remain essential.


6. True or False: All education will be replaced by technology.

Answer: False. The text explicitly states that technology should be used to "enhance learning, not to replace human connection entirely," indicating that traditional methods will continue to play an important role.


7. What does the word "democratized" in paragraph 2 mean?

Answer: In this context, "democratized" means made more accessible to everyone, regardless of their location or economic status. This is supported by the context that students from remote areas can now access courses.


8. What is the author's purpose in writing this text?

Answer: The author's purpose is to inform readers about the transformation of education through technology, highlighting both its benefits and concerns, while recommending a balanced approach.

📌 Practice Text 2: Climate Change

Reading Passage

"Climate change represents one of the greatest challenges facing humanity in the 21st century. The Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era, primarily due to increased greenhouse gas emissions from human activities.

The consequences of this warming are already visible across the globe. Sea levels have risen by about 20 centimeters in the past century, threatening coastal communities. Extreme weather events, including hurricanes, droughts, and floods, have become more frequent and intense. Furthermore, many species of plants and animals are struggling to adapt to rapidly changing environments.

Addressing climate change requires immediate and coordinated action from governments, businesses, and individuals. Transitioning to renewable energy sources, improving energy efficiency, and protecting forests are essential strategies. While the challenge is immense, many experts believe that with sufficient political will and technological innovation, it is still possible to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels."

Questions

1. What is the main idea of the text?

Answer: The main idea is that climate change is a major global challenge caused by human activities, with serious consequences already visible, but that it can still be addressed through coordinated action.


2. By how much has the Earth's temperature risen since the pre-industrial era?

Answer: The Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era.


3. What has caused this temperature increase?

Answer: The temperature increase is primarily due to increased greenhouse gas emissions from human activities.


4. List two consequences of climate change mentioned in the text.

Answer: Two consequences mentioned are: (1) sea levels have risen by about 20 centimeters, threatening coastal communities, and (2) extreme weather events such as hurricanes, droughts, and floods have become more frequent and intense.


5. Why are some species struggling?

Answer: Many species of plants and animals are struggling to adapt to rapidly changing environments caused by climate change.


6. What three strategies are mentioned to address climate change?

Answer: The three strategies mentioned are: (1) transitioning to renewable energy sources, (2) improving energy efficiency, and (3) protecting forests.


7. According to some experts, is it still possible to limit global warming? Why?

Answer: Yes, many experts believe it is still possible to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels, provided there is sufficient political will and technological innovation.


8. True or False: Only governments need to take action on climate change.

Answer: False. The text states that addressing climate change requires immediate and coordinated action from governments, businesses, AND individuals.

📕 10. Final Checklist and Tips for Exam Day

Use this comprehensive checklist to ensure you're fully prepared for the reading comprehension section of the BAC exam.

📌 Before the Exam

📚

Practice with past BAC papers to familiarize yourself with the format and question types

📚

Review all types of questions: global comprehension, detailed, vocabulary, inference

📚

Learn common vocabulary words and their synonyms for academic texts

📚

Understand how to derive word meanings from context

📚

Practice paraphrasing to improve reformulation skills

📚

Time yourself during practice to improve speed

📌 During the Exam: Reading Strategy

// Recommended Reading Sequence
// Step 1: Read the title and first paragraph (1 minute)
This gives you the main topic and author's approach.

// Step 2: Read all questions carefully (2 minutes)
Know what you're looking for before reading the full text.

// Step 3: Read the entire text once (5-7 minutes)
Get a general understanding. Don't stop for unknown words.

// Step 4: Answer questions in order (15-20 minutes)
Start with easier questions. Use the text to find answers.

// Step 5: Review your answers (3-5 minutes)
Check for completeness and accuracy.

📌 Answering Strategy Checklist

✅ For Each Question, Verify:

□ Did I understand what the question is asking?

□ Did I look for the relevant part of the text?

□ Did I base my answer on the text, not on outside knowledge?

□ Did I paraphrase or use my own words where appropriate?

□ Did I include justification for True/False answers?

□ Is my answer complete and clear?

□ Are there any spelling or grammar errors?

📌 Question-Specific Tips

Question Type Key Tips
Global comprehension Think about the main topic and purpose. Read the first and last paragraphs carefully.
Detailed questions Use keywords to locate information. Look for exact matches or paraphrases.
Vocabulary questions Use context clues. Don't rely on dictionary meanings alone.
True/False Always justify with evidence from the text. Watch for NOT, never, always.
Multiple choice Eliminate wrong answers first. Choose the best option, not an perfect one.
Inference Read between the lines. Use evidence to support your conclusions.

📌 Time Management

Activity Recommended Time
Initial reading 5-7 minutes
Answering all questions 15-20 minutes
Review and check 3-5 minutes
Total 25-30 minutes

⚠️ Final Reminders

• Read the instructions carefully — Make sure you understand what is required

• Answer every question — Even if unsure, make an educated guess

• Don't leave blank — All questions deserve an attempt

• Stay calm — If you don't understand something, move on and come back

• Trust your first instinct — Usually correct unless you find clear evidence otherwise

• Write legibly — Illegible answers won't be graded

💡 Final Motivational Tip

Remember: the BAC reading comprehension section is designed to test your ability to understand and interpret texts. With thorough preparation using these strategies, you are well-equipped to tackle any text confidently. Practice regularly, stay focused during the exam, and trust your preparation!
